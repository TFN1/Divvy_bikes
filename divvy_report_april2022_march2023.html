Introduction

Company background

“Divvy is Chicagoland’s bike share system across Chicago and Evanston. Divvy provides residents and visitors with a convenient, fun and affordable transportation option for getting around and exploring Chicago.”

To hire a Divvy bike customers can purchase an annual membership or pay casually via single ride or day passes.

Future of Divvy

The following scenarios are fictional

Divvy’s stakeholders believe that the success of the company depends on maximising the number of annual memberships due to their financial benefit. Rather than creating a marketing campaign to attract new riders, Divvy’s stakeholders want to focus on converting casual riders into members.

Business task

To design a new marketing strategy aimed at converting casual riders into annual members, Divvy’s marketing analyst team needs to understand how annual members and casual riders differ. Insights into how annual members and casual riders are different will help the company make data driven decisions.

“How did member rides and casual rides on Divvy bikes differ in the past 12 months?”

Data

Divvy generously open sources their internal historical trip data to the public. The data has been provided and used under the Divvy Data License Agreement. It is released on a monthly schedule and can be downloaded from the Divvy bikes webpage in csv format. This data is reliable, original, comprehensive, current and cited.

The data is anonymised and provides details on each trip that took place in the given time period. This includes the:

  • Rider type
  • Start date and time
  • End date and time
  • Type of bike
  • Start location
  • End location

# data manipulation; data types, new variables, rename
manipulated_data <- raw_data %>%
  rename(rider_type = member_casual,
         bike_type = rideable_type) %>% 
  mutate(
    bike_type = as.factor(bike_type),
    started_at = ymd_hms(started_at),
    ended_at = ymd_hms(ended_at),
    rider_type = as.factor(rider_type),
    ride_length = as.numeric(ended_at - started_at),
    ride_length_minutes = ride_length/60,
    ride_length_interval = cut(ride_length_minutes, breaks = c(0, 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, Inf), labels = c("0-5 minutes", "5-10 minutes", "10-15 minutes", "15-20 minutes", "20-25 minutes", "25-30 minutes", "30+ minutes")),
    ride_date = date(started_at),
    ride_hour = hour(started_at),
    ride_time = cut(ride_hour, breaks = c(0, 6, 12, 18, 24), labels = c("Early morning", "Morning", "Afternoon", "Evening"), include.lowest = TRUE),
    ride_day = wday(started_at, label = TRUE, week_start = 1),
    ride_day_type = as.factor(ifelse(ride_day %in% c("Sat", "Sun"), "Weekend", "Weekday")),
    ride_month = as.factor(month.name[month(started_at)]),
    route = paste(start_station_name, "to", end_station_name))

The first thing that catches my eye in the summary above is the end latitude and longitude. These variables have missing data (indicated by NA’s) as well as data points at 0, not what they should be.

The next variable I notice is ride length. A few things to note here; this data is recorded in seconds, so the max value of 2,484,335 equates to a ride almost 29 days long, something tells me that ain’t right. At the same time our minimum value is in the negatives which is just not possible, you cannot ride for a negative amount of time.

Finally, let’s check for any duplicate rows.

# check for duplicate columns
data.table::setDT(manipulated_data) %>%
  .[duplicated(.)] %>%
  nrow()
[1] 0

No duplicates is exactly what we want to see.

So to clean out data we will take out observations with incorrect location data and ride length.

Now ride length is a somewhat subjective decision, I have decided to filter out rides under a minute and over 10 hours. Where you draw the line on max length is up for debate, I figured that feasibly someone could ride for 10 hours.

Let’s make these changes and get into analysing the data.

# make appropriate changes
data_set <- manipulated_data %>%
  filter(!is.na(end_lat),
         !is.na(end_lng),
         end_lat > 0.00,
         end_lng < 0.00,
         between(ride_length, 60, 36000))

Insights

So, “how did member rides and casual rides using Divvy bikes differ in the past 12 months?”

  • There were around 1/3 more member rides than casual rides

  • The number of member rides were consistent throughout the year, occurring more often throughout the week at commuting hours

  • The number of casual rides were more volatile throughout the year, peaking during summer and weekends during the middle of the day

  • Across the year member rides lasted close to 1/2 the length of casual rides.

  • Member ride length stayed very consistent throughout the average week and day. Casual ride length peaked on weekends and during the middle of the day.

  • Member rides saw an even split on classic vs electric bikes. Casual rides saw closer to 40% classic bike usage.

So while not all rides fit into the following stereotypes; there are certainly trends that indicate your typical annual member who takes shorter trips to commute to work throughout the whole year. And a typical casual rider who goes on longer rides on summer weekends.

Concluding thoughts

This analysis provides a general understanding of the two types of riders that use Divvy bikes, casuals and members, in the past 12 months. To target the conversion of casual riders into annual members further analysis would need to confirm a subset of casual riders who use Divvy bikes like a member would. There is no point wasting time and energy to try to get a true casual rider to be an annual member, there is a reason these two payment options exist.

An important addition in order to do so, and one of the main limitations of this analysis; is the lack of rider ID. Being able to understand each rider’s individual behaviour including the number of rides they took, the length and location would lead to a greater understanding of how riders use Divvy bikes. This could still be done anonymously by assigning an anonymous rider ID similar to the ride ID. Without this, only broad trends about rider type behaviour can be identified.
